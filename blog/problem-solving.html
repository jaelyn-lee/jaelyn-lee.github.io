Tell your non-tech friend a story about a time you:

1. were blocked on a simple problem

When I was learning the JavaScript Intro, I couldn't understand the basic concept of JS. I felt so frustrated and I even wanted to give up. But one of my best friends who is working as a senior software engineer video called me, brought paper and pen and started explaining the question I was stuck on in a very easy way. And I started understanding how the loops and conditionals work and applying it to other questions. After that, I wrote down the definition and easy examples on paper and use the Rubber Duck method for myself. I explain it as if I am talking to 4 year old and then, the concepts that seemed difficult began to be gradually understood, and through reviewing JS basics, it was possible to organize the concepts more clearly.

2. elegantly solved a problem

While solving this Kata challenge, the problems were not solved and there were errors dozens of times everyday. But instead of giving up, I constantly searched and tried various methods to find answers to solve the problems. The sense of accomplishment when I solved the problems was really great, and it seems to be a force to keep coding. And also the problem that cannot be solved was answered by asking questions to peers and facilitators through discord, and it was very helpful.

3. Reflect on how confident you feel using each of these problem-solving techniques/processes

Pseudocode

I am pretty confident using the Pseudocode as it allows me to organise ideas that I can't organise in my head and organises the order in which code I should write first.

Trying something

I was afraid that I broke the code, so I just copied the existing code from Google and commented out the code that making errors instead of deleting and restarting it because I didn't want to lose it. But I realised that if I don't try, I will never make it. So if I want to try methods or different code orders, I just did it. And sometimes it worked and sometimes it didn't. But I still found it very fun and exciting.

Rubber ducky method

This method was very useful when I do not understand the concept of coding. I am a person who can only begin the practical application if the concept is fully understood by the brain. However, when I entered the application right away in Sprint 3, I was really confused and I couldn't think logically. When I felt so frustrated and had no progress, I listened to David's tech session, and I started to write them all down. After the session, I began to explain them one by one as if I were explaining them to Rubber Duck. I searched again and wrote down the parts that I didn't understand or couldn't explain properly and explained them again. Through this method, the concept of JavaScript was clearly grasped, and coding began to be enjoyable as I began to understand parts that seemed too difficult, such as method, syntax and parameters.

Reading error messages

It actually gives me a great idea of what I need to look at and fix.

Console.logging

I use this method when I practice the exercises or challenges that have been completed before to review them. And I test a small code before I combine different codes or functions to check if it is the right code.

Googling

Google is my best friend and I can't imagine myself without googling. And since I started this course, I can see my googling skill has been improved a lot.

Asking your peers for help

It is definitely helpful but sometimes I feel I am the only one who is very behind and slow. But we all have different backgrounds and knowledge so I am very thankful when they are giving me help.

Asking coaches for help

They are super genius and kind. Sometimes they only give me vague answers, but it definitely makes me grow up.

Improving your process with reflection

Yes, this is my favourite problem-solving method. I was able to gain confidence and get clear answers that I had never thought of before by trying and solving parts that I was not confident in.

4. Reflect on a time you were reluctant to ask for help. Consider what made you reluctant to do so

I think I am too self-conscious about what people think of me. When no one asked for help with the problem I was struggling with, I felt like an idiot who couldn't solve this problem, and I was frustrated because of this. But now it doesn't matter at all and I can ask for help because I will eventually be able to give answers to others.
